Using Anki for Patristic Vocabulary

A companion guide to the 285-term lexicon deck

How to install Anki, import the Patristic Lexicon deck, and weave daily vocabulary review into your 40-week formation — without it becoming another thing to dread.

Why Anki

Anki is a free, open-source spaced-repetition application that has quietly become the standard tool among seminarians, medical students, and language learners for the slow work of committing a vocabulary to long-term memory. It has been under active development for more than fifteen years, runs on every major platform, and costs nothing on desktop and Android.

Orthodox Companion Studies uses Anki rather than building its own in-app flashcard feature for two reasons. First, Anki is already better at this than anything we could build in a reasonable timeframe — its review algorithm has been refined over years of real use. Second, it lets us keep our engineering focus on formational depth: lexicon content, Father card scholarship, biblical companion alignment. Anki owns the flashcard layer; we own the patristic content.

The Patristic Lexicon deck is optional. If you are reading the Fathers in English and absorbing the terms contextually, you can safely skip it. If you want the vocabulary to become automatic — recognized at sight, recalled under pressure — daily Anki review is the tool.

How to Get Anki

Anki runs on every platform. Start with the desktop application; the mobile apps sync with it through a free AnkiWeb account.

Desktop
Free — macOS, Windows, Linux
Download from apps.ankiweb.net. This is where you will import the deck.
AnkiDroid
Free — Android
Install from the Google Play Store. Maintained by a separate open-source team from the desktop app, but fully compatible.
AnkiMobile
$25 one-time — iOS
Optional. Revenue from AnkiMobile funds ongoing development of the free desktop and Android apps. On iPhone or iPad, this is the only Anki app to trust.

An AnkiWeb account is free and lets your review progress sync across devices. Recommended if you plan to review on both desktop and phone. Create one at ankiweb.net and sign in from each device.

Importing the Deck

The deck file is patristic-lexicon.apkg — a single package containing all 285 entries, card templates, and styling.

  1. Download patristic-lexicon.apkg from the Study Tools section on your dashboard. It will land in your Downloads folder.
  2. Double-click the file. Anki opens and imports the deck automatically. If double-click does nothing, open Anki first and use File → Import, then select the file.
  3. After import you will see Patristic Lexicon at the top level of your deck list, with thirteen phase subdecks underneath (Phase 01 through Phase 13, with Phase 02.5 sitting between 02 and 03 — the Tertullian and Cyprian Latin material).
  4. If you are syncing: click Sync once in desktop Anki. The deck uploads to AnkiWeb and becomes available to pull down on your mobile device.

Daily Review

Anki shows you a small batch of cards each day — new cards you have never seen, plus older cards the algorithm has decided are due for review. You look at the front of each card, try to recall the answer, click to flip, then rate your recall. The algorithm uses your ratings to decide when to show the card again.

Again
You did not recall it. The card returns within minutes.
Hard
You recalled it, but it was a struggle. The interval shrinks.
Good
You recalled it cleanly. Use this for most cards — it is the default.
Easy
Trivial recall. The interval grows quickly. Use sparingly; over-using Easy makes cards disappear for months and your memory suffers.

Every lexicon entry generates two cards:

Recognition
Greek or Latin headword on the front, English and full definition on the back. This is the easier direction — appropriate for reading the Fathers.
Production
English gloss on the front, Greek or Latin headword on the back. Harder — appropriate for actually writing or speaking the terms. If Production cards feel like grinding after a week, you can suspend them in bulk (Browse → filter by card template Production → Suspend) and keep only Recognition.

You can review the parent deck to see everything due across all phases, or drill into a single phase subdeck — for example, reviewing only Phase 01 during the weeks you are reading the Apostolic Fathers. Both approaches work; both preserve the same review history.

Tags are applied to every card: phase-1 through phase-13 (including phase-2-5), language-greek and language-latin, father-ignatius-of-antioch and so on for each Father (nineteen distinct), and pastoral-note for the ten entries that carry a pastoral framing in the lexicon. These let you build filtered decks for targeted review — for instance, all Cyril of Alexandria terms, or all Latin terms, or everything from Phase 10 at once.

When the Lexicon Grows

The Patristic Lexicon is a living reference. As new terms are added — the target is eight hundred entries over time — the Anki deck is regenerated and reposted to the dashboard. To update: download the new .apkg and import it the same way as the first time. Anki recognizes existing cards by a stable identifier and updates them in place rather than duplicating; your review history for every term you have already seen is preserved.

If you ever want to reset and start fresh, delete the deck in Anki (right-click the deck → Delete) before re-importing.
