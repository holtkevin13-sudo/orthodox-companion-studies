Photios the Great

Φώτιος ὁ Μέγας · Patriarch of Constantinople
c. 810 – 893 AD · Scholar-Patriarch, Defender of Orthodox Pneumatology

Byzantine aristocrat, imperial secretary, layman-scholar consecrated directly to the Patriarchate of Constantinople in 858, deposed and restored and deposed again across two patriarchates, author of the Mystagogy of the Holy Spirit — the classical Eastern theological response to the Latin addition of the filioque to the Creed — and compiler of the Bibliotheca, a summary of 280 ancient Greek works that preserves our knowledge of many texts otherwise lost. As Patriarch he dispatched the missionary brothers Constantine (Cyril) and Methodius to the Slavs in 863, an act whose consequences for Orthodox Christianity are incalculable. His 867 Encyclical to the Eastern Patriarchs is the first formal Eastern doctrinal protest against the filioque, and the theological center of gravity it articulated has shaped Orthodox pneumatology ever since. Commemorated February 6.

Who Was Photios?

Photios was born in Constantinople around 810 AD into the highest aristocracy of the Byzantine capital. His father Sergios was a cousin of the Patriarch Tarasios (who had presided over the Seventh Ecumenical Council and the restoration of the icons in 787), and his mother's family was equally distinguished. The family was devoutly iconophile during the second iconoclastic persecution of 814–842, and Photios's uncle, also named Sergios, was exiled and imprisoned for his defense of the images. Photios grew up in that atmosphere of educated Orthodox resistance, received the most complete humanist education the ninth century could offer, and as a young man was already considered one of the most learned men in the capital.

He rose through imperial civil service to the office of protoasekretis — chief imperial secretary — while pursuing a parallel private career as philosophical and philological scholar. The Bibliotheca, also called the Myriobiblos ("Ten Thousand Books"), was composed during these early years: two hundred eighty summaries of ancient Greek works that Photios had read and wished his brother Tarasios, sent on an imperial embassy, to have access to in absentia. For many classical and patristic texts, Photios's summaries are the only surviving witness to the now-lost originals. The Bibliotheca alone would have secured his reputation as one of the greatest Byzantine scholars of any century.

In 858 the Emperor Michael III and his regent uncle Bardas deposed the serving Patriarch Ignatius over a political dispute and elevated Photios — still a layman — to the patriarchal throne. He was ordained through all the clerical grades in six days and consecrated on Christmas Day 858. The deposition of Ignatius and the elevation of Photios provoked a protracted controversy with Rome, where Pope Nicholas I refused to recognize the new patriarch. Photios responded in 867 with the Encyclical to the Eastern Patriarchs, which named the filioque — the Latin addition of "and from the Son" to the Creed's article on the procession of the Holy Spirit — as the principal doctrinal innovation of the Frankish missionaries then active in Bulgaria. Within months Emperor Michael III was assassinated, the new Emperor Basil I deposed Photios, and Ignatius was restored. Photios lived in exile for nearly a decade, teaching and writing; when Ignatius died in 877, Basil recalled Photios to a second patriarchate that lasted until 886, when Basil's successor Leo VI deposed him again. Photios retired to the monastery of Armeniakon and died there around 893.

His theological contribution must be read against the whole arc of his patriarchal career. The Mystagogy of the Holy Spirit, composed during his second patriarchate or shortly after his second deposition, articulated the Eastern theological case against the filioque with a rigor that no subsequent Orthodox treatment of the question has substantially surpassed. His dispatch of Cyril and Methodius to Great Moravia in 863 opened the Orthodox Church to the Slavic peoples and established the principle — against the Latin missionary assumption that liturgy must be in Latin — that the Gospel could be preached and worshiped in every language. His scholarly work preserved substantial portions of Greek patristic and classical literature for transmission. The Photian patriarchate was not the beginning of the East-West schism (which culminated in 1054), but it was the first great articulation of what was genuinely at stake between the two traditions. Modern Orthodox scholarship — Francis Dvornik's monumental rehabilitation in the mid-twentieth century, John Meyendorff, Aristeides Papadakis, and more recently Tia Kolbaba — has restored Photios to his proper stature after centuries in which polemical Western historiography had treated him primarily as a schismatic figure.

Major Works

Photios's literary output is enormous and falls into three clusters: scholarly-encyclopedic, exegetical-theological, and correspondence. Three works anchor the reception.

Mystagogy of the Holy Spirit

Περὶ τῆς τοῦ Ἁγίου Πνεύματος Μυσταγωγίας

The classical Eastern theological treatise against the filioque, composed in the 880s. Ninety-five chapters arguing from Scripture, the Creed, the Councils, and the Fathers that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone, and that any dyadic principle of procession ("from the Father and the Son") compromises both the monarchy of the Father and the unity of the divine essence. The work remains the Orthodox theological foundation of every subsequent East-West discussion of the question, from Mark of Ephesus at Florence in 1438 to the modern Catholic-Orthodox dialogue.

The Bibliotheca

Μυριόβιβλος

Also called the Myriobiblos ("Ten Thousand Books"). Two hundred eighty chapters summarizing ancient Greek works — Christian and pagan, patristic, philosophical, historical, medical, grammatical — that Photios had read in his scholarly youth. Many of the works summarized are now lost in their original form, and Photios's summaries are their only surviving witness. The Bibliotheca is one of the greatest single acts of cultural transmission in late-antique or Byzantine scholarship.

Amphilochia

Ἀμφιλόχια

Three hundred and twenty-four theological, exegetical, and philological questions and answers, addressed to Amphilochius of Cyzicus and composed during Photios's exile after his first deposition. The work ranges across Scripture, the councils, doctrinal difficulties, philological questions about rare Greek vocabulary, and problems in canon law. It is one of the key witnesses to the scope of Photian theological scholarship and to the intellectual vitality of ninth-century Byzantine Orthodoxy.

Theological Pillars

Four Greek terms name the center of Photios's theological contribution. The first is the technical term for his central claim about the Holy Spirit; the second the underlying Trinitarian principle; the third his methodological signature; the fourth his appeal-ground against Western innovations.

ἐκπόρευσις
ekporeusis
Procession · From the Father Alone

The technical Greek theological term for the Holy Spirit's eternal procession, which Photios distinguishes sharply from any secondary sending or mission. In his usage ἐκπόρευσις names the Spirit's unique eternal origination from the Father alone — the causal procession — as distinct from the Spirit's temporal sending through the Son into the economy of salvation. The Latin processio collapses the two meanings; Photios preserves the distinction, and the Greek patristic tradition from Gregory of Nyssa and John of Damascus forward stands behind the preservation. The filioque, on Photios's reading, confuses what the Fathers had kept distinct.

μοναρχία
monarchia
Monarchy of the Father · Single Divine Source

The Trinitarian principle underlying Photios's theology of procession: the Father alone is the one unoriginate source of divinity, from whom the Son is eternally begotten and from whom the Spirit eternally proceeds. The filioque, by making the Son a co-cause of the Spirit's procession, compromises the monarchy of the Father and introduces (Photios argues) two principles into the Godhead where there must be only one. The principle is not Photios's innovation — it is the Cappadocian teaching, inherited through Maximus and Damascus — but Photios gives it its sharpest polemical articulation.

μυσταγωγία
mystagōgia
Mystagogy · Initiation into the Mystery

The title category of Photios's great pneumatological work: theology as initiation into mystery rather than as discursive explanation of it. The category anchors the Orthodox conviction that doctrinal theology is inseparable from the liturgical and sacramental life in which the Triune God is actually encountered. Photios writes against the filioque not primarily as a philosophical error but as a liturgical corruption — an alteration of the baptismal Creed that compromises the Church's mystagogical integrity.

παράδοσις
paradosis
Tradition · the Received Patristic Deposit

The appeal-ground of Photios's theology. Παράδοσις is the handed-on apostolic and patristic teaching which no local church has authority to alter unilaterally. The filioque, inserted into the Nicene- Constantinopolitan Creed by local Western councils without ecumenical consent, violates the principle at its root. The argument is not primarily that the Latin theology of procession is wrong (though Photios thinks it is) but that the unilateral alteration of a conciliar Creed is a breach of the conciliar principle of catholic teaching. The appeal-ground has shaped every subsequent Orthodox engagement with Western doctrinal development.

"The Father alone is cause: the Son begotten of him, the Spirit proceeding from him. To confess two causes in the Godhead is to lose the oneness of the divine monarchy."

— Mystagogy of the Holy Spirit 36 (adapted)

Why Photios Matters Today

He is the theologian who gave the Orthodox East its settled answer on the filioque. Every subsequent Eastern engagement with the question — the 1054 mutual anathemas, the Council of Lyon in 1274, the Council of Ferrara- Florence in 1438–1439 where Mark of Ephesus would refuse to sign the union document, and the modern Catholic-Orthodox dialogue inaugurated by the Ravenna Statement and the 2003 North American Statement on the filioque — has been carried out in the theological grammar Photios established. A Byzantine aristocrat who held the patriarchate briefly and twice gave the Orthodox tradition its permanent vocabulary for what the procession of the Spirit does and does not mean.

His dispatch of Cyril and Methodius to the Slavs in 863 is perhaps the single most consequential decision of his patriarchate and one of the great acts of Christian missionary vision in any century. Against the Frankish-Latin missionary assumption that the Gospel must be preached and worshiped only in the three "sacred" languages of Hebrew, Greek, and Latin, Photios and his brothers articulated the principle that every people has the right to hear the Gospel and worship in its own tongue. The principle gave the Orthodox Church its characteristic polyphonic liturgical inheritance — Greek, Slavonic, Romanian, Georgian, Arabic, and eventually English, Spanish, Korean, and many more — and it remains one of the Orthodox tradition's most distinctive gifts to the ecumenical conversation about inculturation and missionary theology.

And his scholarly legacy is worth naming on its own terms. The Bibliotheca's 280 book summaries preserve substantial portions of Greek Christian and pagan literature that would otherwise have been lost to history — the works of the second-century Gnostic apologists, numerous historical chronicles, obscure patristic writers the direct manuscript tradition did not carry forward. When a modern Orthodox scholar cites a second- or third-century author known only through Photios, she is drawing on the cultural memory a ninth-century Byzantine patriarch made it his personal labor to preserve. The pattern — theology and pastoral office undergirded by serious scholarly formation — is a Photian inheritance the modern Orthodox Church continues to need, and continues, in its best moments, to honor.
