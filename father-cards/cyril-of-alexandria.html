Cyril of Alexandria

Κύριλλος Ἀλεξανδρείας · Pillar of the Faith
c. 376 – 444 AD · Patriarch of Alexandria, Defender of the Theotokos

The decisive Christological theologian of the fifth century. Patriarch of Alexandria for thirty-two years, Cyril confronted the preaching of Nestorius at Constantinople, presided at the Council of Ephesus in 431, and vindicated the title Θεοτόκος — "God-bearer" — as guardian of the unity of Christ. His Letters to Nestorius and On the Unity of Christ provided the grammar of hypostatic union that the Council of Chalcedon (451) and every subsequent Orthodox Christology would ratify.

Who Was Cyril?

Cyril was born around 376 AD, probably in a village of the Egyptian Delta. He was the nephew of Theophilus, the powerful and combative Patriarch of Alexandria (385–412), and grew up in and around the patriarchate: educated in its schools, trained in its theological tradition, and present at its political battles. He was with his uncle at the Synod of the Oak in 403 that deposed John Chrysostom — a piece of his biography he would later regret and, after Chrysostom's posthumous rehabilitation, publicly honor by reinserting Chrysostom's name into the diptychs of Alexandria. When Theophilus died in 412, Cyril succeeded him as Patriarch after a brief contested election.

The first years of his episcopate were turbulent in ways that have shadowed his reputation ever after. Socrates Scholasticus reports that Cyril drove the Jewish community out of Alexandria in 414 following mutual violence; he closed Novatianist churches; and in 415 the philosopher Hypatia was lynched by a mob identified with Cyril's followers — precisely how far Cyril himself authorized or inflamed the attack remains genuinely disputed by historians. What is not disputed is that his Alexandria was a hard place, and that his own combative temperament contributed to the hardness. Modern Orthodox theology has tended to look these passages of his biography in the eye rather than around them.

The doctrinal work for which the Church holds him took shape from 428, when Nestorius became Patriarch of Constantinople and began objecting to the title Θεοτόκος. Cyril's letters to Nestorius in 429 and 430 — especially the Second and Third Letters — are the most important Christological documents of the fifth century. The Third Letter concluded with Twelve Anathemas enumerating the errors to be repudiated. The Council of Ephesus, convened at Theodosius II's call in the summer of 431, deposed Nestorius and vindicated Cyril's Christology and the title Θεοτόκος. A two-year schism with the Antiochene party followed; the Formula of Reunion of 433, in which Cyril moderated his language and John of Antioch affirmed Ephesus, reconciled the churches. Cyril spent his final years in pastoral labor and further theological writing — the late dialogue On the Unity of Christ is the summary of his mature mind. He died on June 27, 444 AD. The Orthodox Church commemorates him on June 9 (his repose) and January 18 (with Athanasius).

Major Works

Cyril wrote voluminously across exegetical, dogmatic, and polemical registers — commentary on most of the Old Testament and the Gospels of John and Luke, dogmatic treatises against Arianism in his early period and against Nestorianism later, letters, festal epistles, and apologetic works. Three stand at the center.

Letters to Nestorius

Ἐπιστολαὶ πρὸς Νεστόριον

The Second Letter (early 430) and the Third Letter (November 430) are the dogmatic heart of Cyril's Christology. The Second Letter — formally received by the Council of Ephesus as the standard of orthodoxy — sets out the hypostatic union in its classical form: the one incarnate Son, divine and human in his one subject, of one essence with the Father and of one essence with us. The Third Letter, written after Cyril's patience with Nestorius had run out, concludes with the Twelve Anathemas against each position he identifies as fatal to orthodox faith.

On the Unity of Christ

Ὅτι εἷς ὁ Χριστός

A late dialogue composed around 438 AD, and the single clearest summary of Cyril's mature Christology. Cast as a conversation between Cyril and a friend objecting in good faith, the work patiently works through every objection to a fully unified Christ: the one Son is one and the same before and after the Incarnation; he who hungered is he who sustains the universe; Mary bore God in the flesh. Modern English readers typically encounter Cyril first through this treatise, and the SVS Popular Patristics translation is a standard.

Commentary on John

Εἰς τὸ κατὰ Ἰωάννην

Cyril's longest surviving exegetical work and a major theological source in its own right, composed in the decade before the Nestorian controversy began. The twelve books trace Cyril's mature doctrine of Christ, the Trinity, and the Eucharist through patient verse-by-verse exposition. The commentary is the theological seedbed from which the polemical work against Nestorius later grows — and, for Orthodox eucharistic theology, one of the foundational texts.

Theological Pillars

Cyril's theological vocabulary is almost entirely Christological. Four terms carry the central load — one Marian-Christological, one structural, one logical, one existential — and together express what his whole episcopate contended for: that the Christ of the Gospels is a single subject, fully God and fully human, not a pair of agents somehow conjoined.

Θεοτόκος
Theotokos
God-Bearer · Mother of God

The ancient Marian title that becomes the battle cry of Ephesus. Cyril defends Θεοτόκος against Nestorius's preferred Χριστοτόκος not as a matter of Mariology but of Christology: Mary did not bear a human Jesus later joined to the Word; she bore the one incarnate Word himself. The title safeguards the unity of Christ's single subject.

ἕνωσις καθ' ὑπόστασιν
henōsis kath' hypostasin
Hypostatic Union

Cyril's signature formula. Divinity and humanity in Christ are not merely conjoined (συνάφεια) or co-indwelling (ἐνοίκησις), but united at the level of ὑπόστασις — the one person in whom both natures subsist. The one Christ is one hypostasis in two natures: the grammar that Chalcedon will ratify in 451.

ἀντίδοσις
antidosis
Communication of Attributes

The "exchange of properties" in the one Christ. Because divine and human natures are united in one person, what is proper to each can be predicated of the one Christ: the Son of God truly hungered; the Son of Mary truly created. The predicate does not migrate between natures but is said of the one person in whom both subsist — the logic behind every liturgical phrase like "the Son of God was crucified."

ἰδιοποίησις
idiopoiēsis
Appropriation · Making-One's-Own

The Word's making human experience genuinely his own. The Son takes to himself — as truly his — the hunger, weariness, ignorance, and death of the human nature he has assumed. What happens to Christ's humanity happens to him, not to a separate human person with whom he is associated. Appropriation is the hypostatic union's existential edge.

"The Word of God suffered in the flesh."

— Third Letter to Nestorius, Anathema 12

Why Cyril Matters Today

Cyril is the theologian whose Christological grammar Orthodox Christians still speak. The formula one person in two natures that the Council of Chalcedon sets out in 451 — the grammar that governs every subsequent Orthodox account of Christ — is substantially his. The Second Council of Constantinople in 553 received his Twelve Anathemas as binding; the Fathers of every subsequent Ecumenical Council through Nicaea II treat him as the measure of Christological orthodoxy. When an Orthodox Christian confesses one Christ who is both truly God and truly human and who is one single subject from the womb of the Theotokos — they are confessing Cyril.

His Θεοτόκος is the foundation of the whole Eastern theology of Mary. "Mother of God" is not a Marian honorific added to Christology from the outside; for Cyril it is the Christological claim itself in its most compressed form. Every feast of the Mother of God in the Orthodox calendar speaks Ephesus. Every icon of her holding the Child speaks his grammar.

The harder side of Cyril's reception is also worth naming. The Miaphysite tradition of the Oriental Orthodox Churches — Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, Ethiopian, Eritrean — rightly claims him as their chief theologian: his formula μία φύσις τοῦ Λόγου σεσαρκωμένη ("one incarnate nature of the Word") became their preferred language after Chalcedon. Modern Christological dialogue between Eastern Orthodox and Oriental Orthodox churches, since the twentieth century, has largely proceeded by showing that Cyril's substance is common and the remaining difference is terminological. The rapprochement happens through him, not around him. In this and in other ways, Cyril remains at work in the living Church.
