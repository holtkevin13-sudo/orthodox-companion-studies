Athanasius the Great

Ἀθανάσιος ὁ Μέγας · Pillar of Orthodoxy
c. 296 – 373 AD · Bishop of Alexandria, Defender of Nicaea

Bishop of Alexandria for forty-five years and exiled five times by five different emperors, Athanasius spent the central crisis of the fourth century insisting on a single line of the Creed: that the Son is ὁμοούσιος — of the same essence as the Father. His On the Incarnation remains the classic short statement of why Christ had to be God to save us, and his Life of Antony launched the monastic tradition East and West.

Who Was Athanasius?

Athanasius was born around 296 AD in Alexandria — the intellectual capital of the eastern empire, a city of Greek philosophy, Jewish scholarship, Coptic monasticism, and fiercely disputed theology. He grew up in its church under Bishop Alexander and came of age just as the Arian controversy broke open. In 325 AD, still a young deacon of perhaps twenty-seven, he accompanied Alexander to the Council of Nicaea, where he watched the bishops write the word ὁμοούσιος — "of the same essence" — into the Creed to foreclose Arius's claim that the Son was a creature. Three years later Alexander died and the church of Alexandria elected Athanasius to succeed him. He was about thirty-three.

What followed was one of the most extraordinary episcopates in Christian history. For forty-five years Athanasius held the see of Alexandria, but he held it in fact for only about twenty-eight of them: five times he was driven into exile by imperial authorities siding with Arian or Semi-Arian parties — Constantine the Great, Constantius II, Julian the Apostate, and Valens each had a turn — and he spent roughly seventeen years away from his city. He sheltered among the desert monks in Egypt, in Rome under Pope Julius, in Trier, and more than once in hiding within Egypt itself. From these exiles came the bulk of his polemical writing. A popular phrase of later centuries — Athanasius contra mundum, "Athanasius against the world" — captures both the isolation of his position at several points and the doggedness with which he held it.

His defense of Nicaea was not abstract. The logic he pressed again and again was soteriological: if the Son is not truly God, he cannot communicate divine life to creatures; if he cannot communicate divine life, then what Christians call salvation — the remaking of humanity in God's own life — is impossible. The argument of On the Incarnation, written perhaps before Nicaea and perhaps after (the date is disputed), is the positive case behind the polemic. Beyond the doctrinal combat, he was also the biographer of Antony the Great, whose Life he composed around 357 AD, and in his Festal Letter of 367 he issued what is the first surviving list of the twenty-seven books of the New Testament in exactly the form the Church eventually received. He died at Alexandria, at last in peace, on May 2, 373. The Orthodox Church keeps his feast on January 18, with Cyril, and on May 2 as his koimesis.

Major Works

Athanasius wrote across every genre available to a fourth-century bishop — doctrinal treatises, polemical orations, festal and synodical letters, hagiography, apologetics. Three works anchor his legacy.

On the Incarnation

Περὶ τῆς Ἐνανθρωπήσεως τοῦ Λόγου

Athanasius's short masterpiece — fifty-seven chapters of taut, pastoral theology. The human condition is diagnosed as φθορά (corruption), a slide back toward the non-being from which we were called; the only remedy is that the Word himself enter our condition and, from within it, reverse the trajectory by his own ἀφθαρσία (incorruption). The final chapters arrive at the famous line: because the Word became human, we are made divine. The date is disputed — possibly pre-Nicene (c. 318), possibly later — but the theology is seamless with the mature Athanasius of the Arian controversy.

Orations Against the Arians

Κατὰ Ἀρειανῶν Λόγοι

Three long discourses composed during the second and third exiles (c. 340s–350s), with a fourth of disputed authenticity. Where On the Incarnation builds a positive case, the Orations fight step by step through the Arian reading of Scripture. Proverbs 8:22 ("the Lord created me"), Philippians 2, Hebrews 1 and 3 — Athanasius works through each contested passage, insisting that what the Arians read as evidence of the Son's creaturehood refers to the Word's assumed humanity, not to his eternal divine being. The orations are the theological engine room behind the homoousian confession.

Life of Antony

Βίος Ἀντωνίου

Written around 357 AD, the year after Antony the Great's death, this biography presents the Egyptian monk's withdrawal into the desert, his spiritual combat with demons, and his emergence as a theological voice against Arianism at Athanasius's urging. Evagrius of Antioch translated it into Latin within a generation; Augustine reports in Confessions 8 that hearing about Antony's life was pivotal to his own conversion. The Vita Antonii is the foundational document of monastic literature, shaping both Eastern and Western monasticism.

Theological Pillars

Four Greek terms carry the structural load of Athanasius's theology. Together they narrate a single argument: the Incarnate Word, who is of the same essence as the Father, clothes corruptible humanity with his own incorruption so that human beings are made divine.

ἐνανθρώπησις
enanthrōpēsis
Incarnation · Becoming-Human

Athanasius's preferred term for the Incarnation — chosen over σάρκωσις (enfleshment) to emphasize that the Word assumed not merely flesh but complete humanity, body and rational soul alike. Only if the whole human being is taken up by the Word can the whole human being be saved.

ἀφθαρσία
aphtharsia
Incorruption · Imperishability

The freedom from decay that belongs properly to God and, through the Incarnation, is communicated to human nature. On the Incarnation 9 is the decisive passage: the Word clothes mortal flesh with his own ἀφθαρσία, so that what was racing toward dissolution is anchored in the imperishable life of God.

θέωσις
theōsis
Deification · Divinization

The horizon of salvation: human beings really made divine by participation in the divine life. Athanasius himself more often uses the verbal form θεοποίησις — "deification" — but the reality is the same, and θέωσις becomes the standard term through the Cappadocians and Maximus. Athanasius's famous slogan distills the whole move: the Word became human that humans might be made divine.

ὁμοούσιος
homoousios
Of the Same Essence

The decisive word written into the Creed at Nicaea in 325 — that the Son is of the very same οὐσία as the Father, not a lesser or created being. Athanasius defends ὁμοούσιος for forty years against every alternative because the logic of salvation hinges on it: only one who is God by nature can make creatures God by grace.

"He became human that we might be made divine."

— On the Incarnation 54.3

Why Athanasius Matters Today

Athanasius is the hinge between the pre-Nicene and Nicene Church. Every later Trinitarian and Christological controversy is fought on grammar he helped fix. When Orthodox Christians confess that the Son is "of one essence with the Father" at every Divine Liturgy, they are using Athanasius's word, defended for us at the cost of his repeated exile. The Creed is not a dry formula in his mouth; it is the minimum required to keep saying, truthfully, that Christ saves.

His soteriology is the direct development of what Irenaeus began. Where Irenaeus spoke of Christ recapitulating the whole human trajectory from within, Athanasius shows what such recapitulation must cost and produce. "He became what we are that we might become what he is" (Irenaeus, Against Heresies 5.preface) becomes in Athanasius the terse "He became human that we might be made divine" (On the Incarnation 54). The thread then runs forward to the Cappadocians, to Cyril, to Maximus, and eventually to Gregory Palamas — a coherent Greek-patristic tradition of salvation as genuine participation in the divine life, not a legal transaction over against it.

Two other gifts deserve mention. His Festal Letter of 367 AD lists the twenty-seven books of the New Testament in the exact canon the Church would receive — the first surviving statement of that canon in that form, and a reminder that the boundaries of Scripture were set ecclesially, by bishops reading in communion with their churches. And the Life of Antony took monastic theology out of the Egyptian desert and gave it to the whole Mediterranean world; the shape of Orthodox and Catholic monasticism for the next seventeen centuries begins there. A man who was simply bishop of a North African city did all of this while being driven from his see, time after time, for not adjusting the Creed.
