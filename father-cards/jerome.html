Latin Fathers · Nicene Era

Jerome

Hieronymus Stridonensis · Translator of the Vulgate
c. 347 – 420 AD · Biblical Scholar, Ascetic of Bethlehem

Master linguist, priest, secretary to Pope Damasus, ascetic founder of the monastery at Bethlehem beside the Church of the Nativity, and the Latin-speaking Church's greatest biblical scholar. Over the course of roughly thirty years Jerome produced the Vulgata — a Latin Bible translated not from the Septuagint but directly from the Hebrew for the Old Testament and from the best available Greek manuscripts for the New. The achievement remained the standard Latin Bible of the Western Church for more than a thousand years. His voluminous letters, biblical commentaries, and polemical tracts give us a fuller portrait of a Church Father's daily life than any other fourth-century figure. The Orthodox Church commemorates him on June 15.

Who Was Jerome?

Eusebius Hieronymus was born around 347 AD at Stridon, on the border between Dalmatia and Pannonia — somewhere in the eastern Balkans, the exact location now lost. His family was well-to-do and Christian; his father sent him to Rome in his teens for the finest classical education, under the grammarian Aelius Donatus. Jerome became by all accounts a formidable Latinist, absorbed Virgil and Cicero to the bone, and lived the life of an ambitious young provincial in the capital. He was baptized at Rome around 366, in his late teens, after which he began the slow turn from his classical enthusiasms toward the Christian life. The turn was never complete on his own terms; Jerome's conscience would torment him for decades about whether he was more a Ciceronian than a Christian, and the tension produced some of his sharpest writing.

In 373 he set out for the East — first to Antioch, where a severe illness and a famous dream (in which Christ charged him with being "a Ciceronian, not a Christian") crystallized his commitment to the ascetic life; then to the Syrian desert at Chalcis, where he lived as a hermit for two or three years and began learning Hebrew from a Jewish convert. Jerome's Hebrew would become the foundation of everything that followed. He returned to Antioch, was ordained priest, moved on to Constantinople where he studied under Gregory of Nazianzus, and then in 382 was called to Rome as secretary to Pope Damasus. Damasus set him the task that would define his life: to produce a revised, authoritative Latin text of the Gospels and Psalms. The commission grew. Over the next thirty years Jerome would translate the entire Bible into Latin, revisiting the New Testament from the Greek and — scandalously to some — retranslating the Old Testament from the Hebrew rather than from the Septuagint the Church had received in Greek.

After Damasus's death in 384, Jerome left Rome under a cloud — his acerbic criticisms of the Roman clergy and his close spiritual direction of a circle of aristocratic Christian women, particularly the widow Paula and her daughter Eustochium, had made him enemies. He settled with Paula at Bethlehem in 386. There, adjacent to the Church of the Nativity, they founded a double monastery (one community for men, one for women), and there Jerome spent the last thirty-four years of his life translating, writing letters, composing biblical commentaries, and engaging in the ferocious polemical controversies for which he is equally famous. The most consequential was his break with his old friend Rufinus of Aquileia over Origen's theological legacy. Where Rufinus translated Origen's On First Principles into Latin with a sympathetic rendering, Jerome — who had formerly been an Origen enthusiast himself — turned against certain Origenist positions and attacked Rufinus with lacerating savagery. The quarrel ended their friendship and shaped the Latin reception of Origen for centuries. Jerome died at Bethlehem on September 30, 420, having survived Paula by more than a decade and Eustochium by a few years, surrounded by his manuscripts to the end.

Major Works

Jerome's corpus divides into translation, exegesis, and letters. Three works anchor his lasting contribution — the Vulgate, the correspondence, and the historical-literary catalog that is a source for half of what we know about the first four centuries of Christian writers.

The Vulgate

Biblia Vulgata

Jerome's Latin translation of the entire Bible, composed over roughly thirty years from 382 to 405 or later. The New Testament he revised from existing Old Latin versions against the Greek; the Old Testament protocanonical books he translated directly from the Hebrew — his Hebraica veritas, the Hebrew truth. The Psalms he translated three times, the final Hebrew-based version known as the Psalterium iuxta Hebraeos. The Vulgate remained the standard Latin Bible of the Western Church for over a thousand years and was declared the official text at the Council of Trent.

Letters

Epistulae

Some one hundred fifty surviving letters addressed to popes, bishops, monks, widowed aristocrats, scholars, and old friends turned bitter enemies. The correspondence ranges from delicate spiritual direction (Letters 22, 107, and 130 on the formation of consecrated women) to exegetical consultation (Letters 53, 57 on translation theory) to polemical ferocity (the Apology against Rufinus and the correspondence surrounding the Origenist controversy). No patristic correspondence gives a fuller portrait of a Church Father as a working scholar, pastor, and controversialist.

On Illustrious Men

De Viris Illustribus

A catalog of 135 Christian writers from the apostles through Jerome's own contemporaries (including a self-entry), modeled on Suetonius's pagan collection of the same title. For many early Christian authors — particularly obscure pre-Nicene figures — Jerome's brief entry is the only ancient source of information we possess. The work founded the Christian genre of literary history and remained a reference for Western scholarship through the medieval centuries.

Theological Pillars

Four Latin terms name the center of Jerome's contribution to the Christian tradition. The first is his monumental translation; the second his scholarly principle; the third his way of life; the fourth his most consequential preoccupation — the ideal of consecrated virginity, to which he devoted some of his most influential writing.

Vulgata
vul-GA-ta
The Common Latin Bible

The editio vulgata — the "common edition" — is Jerome's Latin Bible that gradually displaced the Old Latin versions across the Western Church. The work represents a translator's judgment call that still shapes biblical scholarship: that the Christian reading of the Old Testament should be anchored in the Hebrew text rather than in its Greek rendering, even though the Greek Septuagint had been the Bible of the Apostles and the Greek Fathers. The choice was contested at the time and remains debated; what is indisputable is the philological labor that produced the result.

Hebraica veritas
he-BRA-i-ca VE-ri-tas
The Hebrew Truth · Scholarly Principle

Jerome's defense of translating the Old Testament from Hebrew rather than from the Septuagint. His reasoning: to know what Scripture says, the scholar must consult the language in which it was first written. The principle has a cost — it distances the Latin Old Testament from the Septuagint received and used by the Apostolic and Greek patristic tradition — and Augustine himself worried about the implications. But it founded Western biblical scholarship as a discipline that takes the source languages seriously, and the modern critical biblical scholarship of both East and West operates in its shadow.

asceticus
as-CE-ti-cus
Ascetic · The Scholar-Monk

Jerome's life — the hermit years in the Syrian desert, the long decades at the Bethlehem monastery — embodies a distinctive Christian ideal: the ascetic as scholar, the monk as translator. For Jerome, silence, fasting, and vigil were not alternatives to intellectual labor but the conditions that made it possible. The life sketched in his letters to the Bethlehem community and in his biographies of Paul of Thebes and Hilarion became the template for Western scholarly monasticism — the shape that would later produce the Benedictine scriptoria and, eventually, the medieval universities.

virginitas
vir-gi-NI-tas
Consecrated Virginity

Jerome's most influential pastoral labor was his spiritual direction of the Roman aristocratic women who pursued the consecrated life — Paula, Eustochium, Marcella, and their circle. Letter 22 to Eustochium and Letter 130 to Demetrias became the standard Latin treatises on virginity and shaped both Western monasticism and the later medieval theology of consecrated life. His zeal was not always balanced, and his polemic against critics of virginity (notably Adversus Jovinianum) sometimes overstated its case. But the tradition of consecrated virginity as a serious vocation in the Western Church traces its form to Jerome's pen.

Why Jerome Matters Today

He is the father of Christian biblical scholarship as a discipline. Before Jerome, Latin Christians read the Bible; with Jerome, they studied it — consulting the Hebrew where it differed from the Greek, weighing variant readings, attending to the grammar of the source languages, tracing the history of textual transmission. Every Orthodox or Catholic or Protestant biblical scholar who has ever sat down with a Hebrew grammar and a critical apparatus is doing work Jerome essentially invented for the Christian tradition. The modern Orthodox biblical renewal — Paul Nadim Tarazi's OCABS scholarship, the patristic recovery in Sources Chrétiennes, Bishop John Behr's work on early Christian reading — operates in the space Jerome opened, even when it reaches different conclusions.

His preservation labor is nearly as consequential as his translation. Jerome was a collector. The Bethlehem library he built with Paula's resources contained Greek manuscripts that have since been lost; his quotations and translations preserve the only Latin access to significant portions of Origen's biblical commentaries, Eusebius's Chronicle, and other works whose Greek originals are gone. Much of what we know about the pre-Nicene Christian writers reaches us because Jerome catalogued them in De Viris Illustribus. He labored, consciously, as a conservator of a tradition he feared the barbarian invasions would destroy.

And his reception is usefully instructive. Jerome was a difficult man — vituperative in controversy, thin-skinned, capable of savage polemic against people who had been his friends, holding grudges across decades. The tradition has received him with clear-eyed awareness of this. He is remembered as a Doctor of the Church and as a saint, but not as a model of temperament; his holiness worked in him alongside real failures of charity. The Orthodox tradition's handling of Jerome — honoring his labor without idealizing his character — is itself a useful pastoral example. The Church has never required its great figures to be pleasant, only to be faithful, and Jerome's faithfulness to the scriptural task God gave him is what the Church has received.
