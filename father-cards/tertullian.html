Tertullian

Tertullianus Afer · Tertullian the African
c. 155 – c. 240 AD · Rhetor of Carthage, First Latin Father

The first major Christian writer to work in Latin, Tertullian coined the theological vocabulary the Western Church has used ever since — trinitas, substantia, persona, sacramentum, regula fidei — and pressed it into polemical, apologetic, and sacramental work of astonishing range. His later drift into Montanism keeps him outside the Church's calendar of saints, but his grammar runs through all subsequent Latin theology.

Who Was Tertullian?

Tertullian was born around 155 AD in Carthage, the great Latin-speaking city of Roman North Africa. Our biographical sources are thin and partly contested — chiefly Jerome's later notices and inferences from Tertullian's own works — but the picture is consistent: a highly educated Roman African, deeply formed in classical rhetoric and the Latin legal tradition, who converted to Christianity around 197 AD. His writings are saturated with the cadences of Roman oratory and legal argument. Whether he was ever formally a presbyter, as Jerome claims, is disputed; many modern readers take him to have been a layman of exceptional theological authority.

From roughly 197 until the 220s he poured out treatises: apologetic works addressed to pagan governors, polemics against Marcion and Praxeas, legal-style arguments for the Church's prior claim to Scripture, catechetical pieces on Baptism and prayer, anthropological and ascetical works, and a theological Christology that already speaks of duae substantiae — two natures — in Christ nearly 250 years before Chalcedon. Across more than thirty surviving treatises he effectively invents Latin theological vocabulary: trinitas, substantia, persona, sacramentum, regula fidei, satisfactio, praescriptio. Whatever one thinks of his later path, the West's theological Latin is Tertullian's Latin.

Around 207 AD Tertullian moved toward the New Prophecy movement from Asia Minor — what later writers called Montanism — with its claims of ongoing prophetic revelation and its rigorist discipline. By his later works he was writing explicitly from within that movement and against the Catholic Church's more lenient pastoral practice on questions of penance, remarriage, and flight in persecution. The Church has never enrolled him among the saints; no Orthodox synaxarion commemorates him. Yet even in antiquity Cyprian, his young admirer in the next generation, is reported to have asked for "the Master" each day and read him as a source — the pattern of critical reception that the Church has applied to Tertullian ever since. His vocabulary is part of the Church's permanent theological inheritance; his Montanist turn is not.

Major Works

Tertullian's corpus is vast and varied — apologetic, polemical, pastoral, ascetical. Three works stand at the heart of his theological legacy and structure the reading in this week: a defense of Christianity to Rome's ruling class, the treatise that gave Latin its Trinitarian grammar, and a legal argument that Scripture belongs to the Church.

Apologeticum

Apologeticum · Apology

Written around 197 AD and addressed to the Roman provincial governors, the Apologeticum is Tertullian's most influential apologetic work. In polished forensic Latin he dismantles the standard legal and moral charges against Christians, defends the Church's worship and common life, and turns the prosecution back on its accusers. The treatise closes with the aphorism that would echo through the history of Christian persecution: the blood of Christians is a seed.

Against Praxeas

Adversus Praxean

Composed c. 213 AD against a Monarchian teacher who collapsed the Trinity into a single divine person, Adversus Praxean is the foundational text of Latin Trinitarian theology. Here Tertullian fixes the grammar that the West will use permanently: una substantia, tres personae — "one substance, three persons." In Christ, he argues, duae substantiae are conjoined in una persona — two natures in one person — anticipating Chalcedon by more than two centuries.

Prescription of Heretics

De Praescriptione Haereticorum

Written around 200 AD, this short treatise deploys a Roman legal term — praescriptio, a prior claim that defeats an opponent's case before it can be argued — to insist that Scripture belongs to the Church. Heretics, Tertullian argues, have no standing to appeal to Scripture because the Apostolic tradition, publicly handed down through the succession of bishops, precedes and circumscribes any heretical reading. Scripture is the Church's book, not the sectarian's.

Theological Pillars

Tertullian is a coiner of words. Where earlier Christians took over existing terms from Greek or Latin culture, he forges new vocabulary or wrenches familiar words into new theological shapes. Four terms carry the weight of his inheritance.

trinitas
Tertullian's coinage
Trinity · Triune God

The first technical theological term for the Triune God in any language (Adversus Praxean 2). Tertullian's formula una substantia, tres personae precedes by more than a century the Greek μία οὐσία, τρεῖς ὑποστάσεις of the Cappadocians and sets the Latin grammar of the Trinity permanently.

persona
cf. πρόσωπον · ὑπόστασις
Person · Distinct Subsistent

Originally the actor's mask or legal standing in Roman life, Tertullian transforms persona into the Latin term for the three distinct subsistents within the one God. The word later becomes contested when Latins translate it into Greek, since πρόσωπον still carried the residue of "mere mask" — a reminder that theological vocabulary travels unevenly across languages.

regula fidei
cf. κανὼν τῆς ἀληθείας
Rule of Faith

The fixed summary of core apostolic teaching, handed down publicly from the Apostles through the bishops. Tertullian invokes the regula fidei throughout his polemical works as the criterion against which heretical novelty is measured — the Latin cognate of Irenaeus's Greek κανὼν τῆς ἀληθείας, and the structural anchor of orthodox reading.

sacramentum
cf. μυστήριον
Sacrament · Sacred Oath

Originally the Roman soldier's oath of allegiance to his emperor. Tertullian applies sacramentum to Baptism above all: the Christian enlists under Christ the King and binds himself to his campaign. The martial resonance stays in the Latin word and shapes Western sacramental theology for a thousand years.

Semen est sanguis Christianorum.

"The blood of Christians is a seed."

— Apologeticum 50.13

Why Tertullian Matters Today

Tertullian hands the Latin Church its theological grammar. Trinitas, substantia, persona, natura, sacramentum, regula fidei, satisfactio — these are not decorative Latinisms but the load-bearing terms of Western theology, and every one of them is either his coinage or his decisive reshaping. When Augustine writes on the Trinity, when Anselm writes on atonement, when the scholastics work out sacramental theology, the vocabulary is still Tertullian's. Orthodox theologians engaging the Latin tradition necessarily pass through him.

His Trinitarian grammar in Adversus Praxean — one substance in three persons, and in Christ two substances conjoined in one person — anticipates by a long margin the formulas that the ecumenical councils will canonize. He is not, of course, Nicene or Chalcedonian in the technical sense; the councils will refine and sometimes correct the Latin vocabulary as it crosses into Greek. But the West receives from Tertullian the grammatical structure into which Nicene and Chalcedonian confession will later be fitted.

His Montanist turn is a genuine complication and the Church has not pretended otherwise. Montanism's rigorism, its claim of ongoing prophecy, and its eventual sectarian posture set Tertullian at odds with the Catholic Church's pastoral practice, and his later works argue that case sharply. The Church's response has been a mature one: to receive his vocabulary and his orthodox theology, to read him as a source and not as a saint, and to let the Montanist material stand as a warning rather than a warrant. The principle at work is Augustine's later one — that the truth is not diminished by the flaws of the one who speaks it — and it is a principle the Orthodox tradition shares.
