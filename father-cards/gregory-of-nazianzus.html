Gregory of Nazianzus

Γρηγόριος ὁ Θεολόγος · The Theologian
c. 329 – 390 AD · Cappadocian Father and Archbishop of Constantinople

The orator, poet, and theologian of the Cappadocian trio — lifelong friend of Basil, briefly Archbishop of Constantinople at the very moment the Church settled her Trinitarian confession, and the only Father besides John the Evangelist (and later Symeon the New Theologian) honored in Orthodox tradition as ὁ Θεολόγος, "The Theologian." His Five Theological Orations, preached at Constantinople in 380, fix the Orthodox grammar for speaking of the Trinity.

Who Was Gregory?

Gregory was born around 329 AD at the family estate of Arianzum near the small Cappadocian town of Nazianzus. His father, also Gregory, was a convert from a Jewish-pagan syncretic sect and had become bishop of Nazianzus; his mother Nonna, his younger brother Caesarius (a court physician), and his sister Gorgonia are all commemorated as saints. He was sent abroad for his education — first to Caesarea Cappadocia, then to Caesarea in Palestine, then to Alexandria, and finally to Athens, where he studied rhetoric and philosophy for almost a decade. It was at Athens that he met Basil. The friendship that began there — two young Cappadocians who, as Gregory later wrote in his funeral oration for Basil, "seemed to share one soul inhabiting two bodies" — became the defining relationship of his life.

Gregory was reluctant about ordained ministry in a way Basil never was. Baptized only in his thirties, he wrote the first treatise in the Christian tradition on the difficulty of the priestly office, Oration 2 On the Priesthood — an immense text on the pastor's duty to purify himself before purifying others, composed after he had fled his own ordination and then returned. When Basil in 372 consecrated him bishop of Sasima — a tiny trafficked crossroads Gregory scornfully described as "a wholly horrid little place" — the act strained their friendship; Gregory never actually served there, assisting his elderly father at Nazianzus instead. After his father's death in 374 he retreated to monastic solitude at Seleucia for several years.

In 379 the small Nicene community at Constantinople — reduced almost to a sect by decades of Arian imperial patronage — invited him to come and preach. From a private chapel he called Anastasia ("Resurrection"), Gregory delivered the homilies that would become the Five Theological Orations (Or. 27–31) in the summer of 380. They rebuilt Nicene orthodoxy in the capital city. When Theodosius I entered Constantinople later that year with an edict mandating Nicene Christianity, Gregory was installed as bishop. He presided at the Council of Constantinople in 381 after the death of Meletius of Antioch. But canonical objections — he had been consecrated for Sasima, not Constantinople — combined with ecclesial politics to push him out, and within weeks he resigned. He retired to Arianzum and spent his last years in prayer, writing, and composing some of the most remarkable autobiographical poetry in the patristic corpus. He died around 390 AD. The Orthodox Church commemorates him on January 25.

Major Works

Gregory's surviving corpus is the most literarily rich of any Greek Father of his century: orations, letters, and a substantial body of Greek hexameter and iambic poetry. Three groups of writings anchor his theological legacy.

Five Theological Orations

Λόγοι Θεολογικοί (Or. 27–31)

The Orthodox Church's classical statement on the Trinity, preached at the Anastasia chapel in Constantinople during the summer of 380. The first oration is a preface on the moral-ascetical prerequisites of theology (κάθαρσις). The second defines theology itself. The third and fourth unfold the deity of the Son; the fifth, the deity of the Holy Spirit. Together they formed the theological spine of the Council of Constantinople (381) and gave Gregory his title: ὁ Θεολόγος.

Festal Orations

Or. 38–41, 45

Oration 38 on the Nativity ("Christ is born, give glory! Christ from heaven, meet him!"), Oration 39 on Theophany, Oration 40 on Holy Baptism, Oration 41 on Pentecost, and Oration 45 on Pascha. These festal sermons set the Orthodox pattern for preaching at the great feasts, and large sections have passed directly into the hymnography of the Byzantine rite. Oration 38 in particular is read at Orthros of the Nativity to this day.

Letters 101 & 102 to Cledonius

Ἐπιστολαὶ πρὸς Κληδόνιον

Two dense letters composed around 381–383 against the Christology of Apollinarius of Laodicea, who had denied Christ a human rational soul. Letter 101 contains Gregory's most famous theological formula — what is not assumed is not healed — the axiom that fixes soteriology to Christology and requires a whole Christ for a whole salvation. The letters are the direct precursor of the Christological work of Cyril and the Chalcedonian settlement.

Theological Pillars

Four Greek terms carry the distinctive weight of Gregory's theology. The first two are Trinitarian, the third Christological, the fourth ascetical — but for Gregory these are not separate departments. Theology is one reality: the purified soul's speech about the triune God who has assumed us wholly in Christ.

Θεολόγος
Theologos
The Theologian

The epithet applied in Orthodox tradition to only three figures: John the Evangelist, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Symeon the New Theologian. That Gregory alone among the post-apostolic Fathers receives this title marks the degree to which his treatment of God's triune being in the Five Theological Orations became the Orthodox grammar for speaking of God.

σχέσις
schesis
Relation · Relationship

What distinguishes the three hypostases of the Trinity from one another, Gregory argues, is not their essence (one) nor their activity (one) but their mutual σχέσεις: the Father is Father only in relation to the Son; the Son is Son only in relation to the Father; the Spirit proceeds from the Father. Trinitarian distinction is relational at root — and therefore neither divides the divine essence nor reduces the three to modes of the one.

ἀπρόσληπτον
aproslēpton
Unassumed · Not Taken Up

Against Apollinarius, who denied Christ a human rational soul, Gregory lays down the decisive axiom of Orthodox Christology: τὸ γὰρ ἀπρόσληπτον, ἀθεράπευτον — "what is not assumed is not healed" (Letter 101.5). Christ's saving work reaches every dimension of human nature precisely because he assumed every dimension. No half-Incarnation could save a whole humanity.

κάθαρσις
katharsis
Purification · Cleansing

The opening move of the first Theological Oration: one must be purified before speaking of God, and even then one must speak with fear (Or. 27.3). Theological speech without κάθαρσις corrupts both speaker and hearer. The principle grounds a long Orthodox tradition joining moral-ascetical discipline to theological competence — you cannot separate how one lives from what one may rightly say about God.

"What is not assumed is not healed."

Why Gregory Matters Today

Gregory is the theologian whose vocabulary Orthodox Christians use whenever they speak of the Trinity in anything beyond the barest terms. His account of the three hypostases as relational — distinguished not by essence or activity but by their mutual σχέσεις — is the grammar every later Trinitarian theology (including Western scholastic thought) picks up and works with. His consolidation of what Basil had begun around οὐσία and ὑπόστασις made possible the 381 creed, and through that creed, the shared confession of all historic Christians.

His Christological dictum is equally foundational. "What is not assumed is not healed" fixes the logic that every later Christological controversy — Nestorian, monophysite, monothelite — has to navigate. If Christ did not assume a human mind, the mind is unhealed; if he did not assume a human will, the will is unhealed. Maximus the Confessor, in his seventh-century fight against monotheletism, will press exactly this Gregorian premise all the way through. The axiom is still operative in every serious modern account of the Incarnation.

But Gregory's witness is not only doctrinal. He is the patristic teacher of a connection Orthodoxy has never let go of: the connection between theology and spiritual life. Theology is not a skill; it is the purified soul's speech about God. To theologize without κάθαρσις, he says, is to corrupt both the speaker and the hearer. This has immense implications for how Orthodox Christianity understands theological education — why the monk and the pastor have some claim to theology that the credentialed intellectual alone does not. The academic theologian who is not praying, Gregory suggests, is doing something other than theology. That critique still stings, and it still guides.
