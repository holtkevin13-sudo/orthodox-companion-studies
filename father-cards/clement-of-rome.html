Clement of Rome

Κλήμης ὁ Ῥωμαῖος · Clement the Roman
c. 35 – c. 99 AD · Bishop of Rome, Hieromartyr

A successor of the Apostle Peter in the Roman see, Clement wrote in the last years of the first century to the church at Corinth, where a schism had unseated the lawful presbyters. His letter — the earliest surviving Christian document outside the New Testament — grounds apostolic succession, ecclesial concord, and the Church's ordered life in the sending of Christ.

Who Was Clement?

Clement stands at the threshold between the apostolic generation and the sub-apostolic Church. Irenaeus, writing around 180 AD, names him as third in the line of Roman bishops after the Apostles — following Linus and Anencletus — and reports that Clement had himself known the Apostles and kept their preaching fresh in his ears. Tertullian gives an alternative tradition in which Peter ordained Clement directly. Whichever sequence one follows, Clement is unmistakably a bridge figure: a Roman Christian of the second Christian generation whose memory and authority the Church at Rome kept close.

The event that fixes him in history is a letter. Around the year 96 AD — near the close of Domitian's reign, or shortly after — the Roman church wrote to the Corinthians to address a grievous situation: a group of younger men had deposed the lawful presbyters of the Corinthian church, apparently without cause. The letter, though formally written in the name of "the Church of God which sojourns at Rome" to "the Church of God which sojourns at Corinth," was understood in antiquity to have been composed by Clement. It was read aloud in the Corinthian liturgy long after it was sent, and some eastern churches treated it as near-canonical well into the fourth century.

Tradition holds that Clement was martyred under Trajan — tied to an anchor and drowned in the Black Sea. The details are late and legendary, but the Church has remembered him as a hieromartyr from an early date, and his name is preserved in the intercessions of the Divine Liturgy. In the ninth century, Saints Cyril and Methodius recovered relics believed to be his and brought them to Rome, where they remain at the Basilica of San Clemente.

Written Legacy

Only one work can be credited to Clement with any confidence. But his name attracted further writings in antiquity, and the whole cluster — authentic, attributed, and pseudepigraphic — shaped how later Christians remembered the earliest Roman Church.

1 Clement (Epistle to the Corinthians)

Πρὸς Κορινθίους Αʹ

Clement's authentic letter, sixty-five chapters long, sent from Rome to Corinth c. 96 AD to heal a schism in which a faction had deposed the rightful presbyters. The letter interweaves Old Testament typology, Pauline echoes, and classical motifs on order and concord to press the Corinthians toward repentance and restoration of their ministers. It is the earliest surviving Christian writing outside the New Testament and the first datable articulation of apostolic succession as an ecclesial principle.

2 Clement (attributed)

Πρὸς Κορινθίους Βʹ

An anonymous homily of the mid-second century, bundled with 1 Clement in the early manuscript tradition and thus preserved under his name. Modern scholarship is unanimous that Clement did not write it, but the text remains valuable as the earliest surviving Christian homily and continues to appear in editions of the Apostolic Fathers — including the Holmes volume used in this program.

Pseudo-Clementine Literature

Ψευδοκλημεντινά

A later Syrian corpus — the Homilies, the Recognitions, and two epistles on virginity — composed in Clement's name in the third and fourth centuries. The texts are valuable for the history of Jewish-Christian theology and early Christian novelistic writing, but they are pseudepigraphic by scholarly consensus and should not be read as Clementine in any strict sense.

Theological Pillars

1 Clement returns again and again to four related concerns: the Church as a divinely ordered community, the concord proper to it, the apostolic sending that constitutes its ministry, and the love in which its whole life is held. These are not four separate topics but four facets of a single vision — the Church as God's household, rightly ordered because given, not negotiated.

ἐκκλησία
ekklesia
Church · Called-out Assembly

For Clement the ἐκκλησία is not a voluntary society but a body given by God, patterned on Israel's ordered worship and bearing the ministry the Apostles handed on. The Corinthian rupture is grave precisely because it tears what has been divinely knit.

ὁμονοία
homonoia
Concord · Like-mindedness

The central virtue of the letter. Clement opposes ὁμονοία to σχίσμα and στάσις — schism and sedition — and draws on both Scripture and the ordered regularity of creation itself to show that concord is not mere agreeableness but the peace God grants to his household.

ἀπόστολος
apostolos
Apostle · Sent One

In chapters 42 and 44 Clement sets out the sending that constitutes ministry: Christ was sent from God; the Apostles were sent from Christ; they appointed the first bishops and deacons; and those first appointed arranged for succession in their turn. This is the earliest surviving articulation of apostolic succession as the Church's structural principle.

ἀγάπη
agape
Love · Self-Giving Love

Chapters 49–50 form an encomium on love in the manner of 1 Corinthians 13, reshaped for a letter about schism. For Clement, love is not sentimental: it is the substance of communion — the virtue that heals division because it is, by its nature, the opposite of division.

"Love admits no schism, love raises no rebellion, love does all things in concord."

— 1 Clement 49.5

Why Clement Matters Today

Clement hands the Orthodox Church a framework that remains load-bearing. The claim in chapter 42 — that apostolic ministry is a line of sending that begins in the Father and reaches the local church through the Apostles and their appointed successors — is the structural logic behind Orthodox ecclesiology. Bishops do not inherit a role they designed; they stand in a chain of hands they did not originate. This is why schism, for Clement, is not a disagreement among Christians but a rupture in a sending.

He also gives the Church a vocabulary for disagreement. Concord (ὁμονοία) is not forced unanimity or conflict avoidance; it is the peace proper to a body whose members have been fitted together by God. Clement's willingness to address the Corinthians' internal crisis from across the Mediterranean — firmly, pastorally, with extensive appeal to Scripture rather than to his own office — models an ecclesial seriousness that is neither authoritarian nor indifferent.

Finally, the letter's presence in the intercessions of the Divine Liturgy keeps Clement's voice inside the Church's prayer. To remember him is to remember a Rome that still belonged unambiguously to the one Church, and to hold in view a form of primacy exercised as service to concord rather than as jurisdiction over it.
