Maximus the Confessor

Μάξιμος ὁ Ὁμολογητής · The Confessor
c. 580 – 662 AD · Byzantine Monk and Theological Synthesizer

Byzantine nobleman turned monk, the most complete theological synthesizer in the patristic tradition, and the confessor who lost his right hand and tongue rather than subscribe to an imperial compromise he believed fatal to the Christian faith. Maximus gathered the Cappadocians, Cyril, and Dionysius into a single luminous vision of God, creation, and the Incarnate Word — and sealed it with his blood. His Ambigua, Two Hundred Chapters, Centuries on Love, and Mystagogy anchor the Orthodox theological imagination to this day.

Who Was Maximus?

Maximus was born around 580 AD. The traditional Greek Life places his birthplace in Constantinople and his family among the Byzantine nobility; a Syriac Life discovered in the early twentieth century gives him Palestinian origins and a less aristocratic background. Scholars still argue the point. What is certain is that by his late twenties he held high administrative office at the imperial court under Emperor Heraclius — protoasecretis, first secretary — and that around 614, at the height of his career, he renounced it. He entered the monastery of Chrysopolis, across the Bosphorus from Constantinople, and later moved to the community of St. George at Cyzicus. He never held ecclesiastical office; he remained, throughout his long life, simply a monk.

The Persian invasion of Asia Minor in 626 drove Maximus west. He reached Carthage, in North Africa, probably around 628, and there he stayed for the next twenty years under the patronage of the monastery of Eucratas and in close association with its abbot Sophronius — the same Sophronius who in 634 would become Patriarch of Jerusalem and open the resistance to Monothelitism. From Carthage, Maximus wrote his greatest works: the two collections of Ambigua (theological "difficulties" drawn from Gregory of Nazianzus and Pseudo-Dionysius), the Four Centuries on Love, the Two Hundred Chapters on Theology and Economy, the Questions to Thalassius, and the Mystagogy. In 645 he publicly debated the deposed Monothelite patriarch Pyrrhus at Carthage and, according to the surviving acts, persuaded him — briefly — of the dyothelite position.

The confrontation with imperial Monothelitism defined his last fifteen years. The Emperor Constans II had issued the Typos (648) forbidding discussion of Christ's wills — a compromise intended to placate Monothelite dissent in Syria and Egypt, and one Maximus judged a betrayal of Chalcedonian Christology. He traveled to Rome. At the Lateran Council of 649, under Pope Martin I, Maximus was almost certainly the principal theological architect of the council's ringing affirmation of two wills and two energies in Christ. For this, both Maximus and Martin were arrested by imperial agents in 653. Martin was exiled to Crimea, where he died in 655. Maximus was tried at Constantinople, exiled to Bizya in Thrace, retried, and finally — in 662, at about eighty-two years of age — condemned to have his right hand cut off (to silence his writing) and his tongue cut out (to silence his confession). He was exiled to Lazica on the east coast of the Black Sea, where he died on August 13 of that same year. Nineteen years later, the Sixth Ecumenical Council at Constantinople (681) vindicated his teaching; the two wills and two energies became binding Orthodox doctrine. The Church commemorates him on January 21.

Major Works

The Maximian corpus is unusually diverse. It includes dogmatic treatises, exegetical commentary, liturgical theology, spiritual handbooks in the aphoristic "centuries" form, and a body of letters and polemical opuscula against Monothelitism. Three works anchor the program.

Ambigua

Περὶ διαφόρων ἀπόρων

Two collections of theological "difficulties." Ambigua to John — the earlier, longer collection — works through knotty passages in Gregory of Nazianzus, developing along the way the classical doctrine of the λόγοι, the cosmic mediation of Christ, and the modes of deification. Ambigua to Thomas addresses Pseudo-Dionysius. Together the Ambigua are Maximus's speculative masterpiece: Cappadocian theology, Dionysian apophasis, and Cyrillian Christology drawn into a single coherent vision.

Two Hundred Chapters on Theology

Κεφάλαια θεολογικὰ καὶ οἰκονομικά

Two hundred aphoristic chapters — arranged, like Evagrius's "centuries" and the later Philokalic collections, in gnomic sentences meant for meditative reading rather than linear argument. The work moves between divine incomprehensibility, the economy of salvation, the ascetic struggle, and the contemplation of nature and Scripture. It is one of the fullest compressed statements of Maximus's theological vision — a primary text for the spiritual reader as much as for the dogmatician.

Disputation with Pyrrhus

Διάλεξις πρὸς Πύρρον

The acts of the public debate held at Carthage in July 645 between Maximus and Pyrrhus, the deposed Monothelite Patriarch of Constantinople. The dialogue preserves Maximus's decisive arguments for two natural wills in Christ — the biblical grounding in Gethsemane, the metaphysical premise that a nature without its natural faculties is no nature at all, the Christological necessity that what is not assumed is not healed. The Disputation became the standard dyothelite reference for the remaining decades of the controversy.

Theological Pillars

Four Greek terms carry the weight of Maximus's witness. The first is the doctrine for which he was mutilated and exiled; the second his signature cosmology; the third a term he was first to use substantively for the mystery of Christ's two natures; the fourth the title the Church gave him for what he endured.

δύο θελήματα
dyo thelēmata
Two Wills · Dyothelite Doctrine

The one Christ, because he has two complete natures, has two natural wills — one divine, one human — operating in perfect harmony. Against Monothelitism, Maximus argues that a nature without its natural faculties is no nature at all: the human Christ prays "not my will but yours" with a genuinely human will, freely willing what the Father wills. The doctrine is vindicated at Constantinople III in 681 — nineteen years after Maximus died in exile.

λόγοι
logoi
Logoi · Divine Reasons

Every creature has its own λόγος — the divine reason according to which it exists and is ordered toward God — eternally held in the mind of the incarnate Λόγος. Creation is the speaking-forth of the one Word into many words; salvation is the drawing of the many λόγοι back into unity in the one. The doctrine is Maximus's signature cosmological synthesis, taking up Stoic, Philonic, and Dionysian lineages into a distinctively Christian vision of cosmic Christology.

περιχώρησις
perichōrēsis
Mutual Indwelling · Coinherence

Interpenetration. Maximus is the first Christian theologian to use περιχώρησις substantively, applying it to the mutual coinherence of the two natures in Christ: divinity and humanity interpenetrate without confusion in the single Son. John of Damascus will extend the term to the Trinity, giving Orthodox Triadology one of its central categories. The grammar is Maximus's.

ὁμολογητής
homologētēs
Confessor

The title the Church gives to one who has suffered for the faith without martyrdom in the strictest sense. Maximus bears it because of what his last decade cost him: condemned by the imperial Monothelite court, tortured, mutilated in his writing hand and his speaking tongue, exiled to die on the far Black Sea. His confession outlived his voice. The Church's title tells what kind of theology this was — theology paid for in the body.

Why Maximus Matters Today

Maximus is the patristic synthesizer — the theologian through whom the prior tradition passes into its durable Byzantine form. Irenaeus's recapitulation, Athanasius's incarnational soteriology, the Cappadocians' Trinitarian grammar, Cyril's Christology, Dionysius's apophatic and cosmological vision: Maximus holds all of it together without reducing any of it. When an Orthodox theologian of any later century reaches for the patristic inheritance, she reaches through Maximus. He is the lens in which the tradition is focused and the channel through which it reaches Palamas, the Philokalic writers, and the modern Orthodox renewal.

His specifically dyothelite doctrine is not a museum piece. To say that Christ willed humanly as well as divinely is to insist that human willing — including the prayer in Gethsemane, "not my will but yours be done" — was genuinely taken up by the Son of God and made his own. Every Christian account of prayer, of obedience, of the Christian's conformity to Christ depends on this being true. If Christ had no human will, the Incarnation did not reach the faculty by which humans most characteristically sin or are saved. Maximus saw this with a clarity no one before him had matched, and the Church paid him the extraordinary compliment of making his answer the Church's answer.

The λόγοι doctrine is the patristic tradition's fullest resource for thinking about creation's relation to God. Each created thing has its divine reason, held eternally in the incarnate Word, and finds its completion by returning to that Word. This is not pantheism — the creature is not God — but it is a cosmology in which every thing bears the imprint of Christ and every thing is ordered toward him. Contemporary Orthodox accounts of ecology, of work, of the arts, all draw from this well. And the Mystagogy's reading of the Divine Liturgy as a cosmic symbol — the Church enacting the whole of creation's return — has shaped every subsequent Orthodox liturgical commentary. Finally, there is his witness itself. That the Church's answer to the most subtle Christological question of its first seven hundred years was defended, at the cost of hand and tongue, by an old monk with no office except his confession — this is itself a patristic teaching. Orthodoxy has never separated theology from the one who suffers for it, and Maximus is why.
