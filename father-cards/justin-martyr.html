Justin Martyr

Ἰουστῖνος ὁ Φιλόσοφος · Justin the Philosopher
c. 100 – c. 165 AD · Philosopher, Apologist, Martyr in Rome

The first major Christian thinker to set the faith in sustained conversation with Greek philosophy, Justin argued that whatever is true in Plato or the Stoics belongs already to Christ, the incarnate Logos in whom all reason shares. His two Apologies and Dialogue with Trypho laid the foundations of Logos Christology, typological exegesis, and the Christian engagement with the wider intellectual world.

Who Was Justin?

Justin was born around 100 AD in Flavia Neapolis — modern Nablus — in Samaria, to a pagan Greek-speaking family. He himself tells the story of his conversion at the opening of the Dialogue with Trypho. As a young man he moved through the philosophical schools of his age in succession: Stoics, Peripatetics, Pythagoreans, and finally Platonists, in whom he thought he had found rest. Then, walking one day by the sea, he met an unnamed old man who pressed him with questions his Platonism could not answer and pointed him to the Hebrew prophets and to Christ. Justin did not abandon philosophy; he changed its object. He continued to wear the philosopher's cloak after his baptism and taught Christianity as the one school of thought that actually delivered what the others sought.

By the middle of the second century he had opened a school in Rome, where he taught openly and debated publicly with rival teachers — among them the Cynic philosopher Crescens, who (according to Justin himself and later tradition) bore a grudge that eventually helped bring him before the authorities. His students included Tatian, who later wrote his own apologetic work and compiled the Diatessaron. Around 165 AD Justin and six of his companions were arrested, tried before Junius Rusticus, urban prefect under Marcus Aurelius, and executed for refusing to sacrifice to the gods. The proceedings survive as the Acts of Justin and Companions, one of the earliest authentic Christian court-transcript martyrdoms.

His three surviving works — the First Apology, the shorter Second Apology, and the lengthy Dialogue with Trypho — are the first substantial body of post-apostolic Christian prose. Eusebius and Irenaeus knew of several more that are now lost: a Syntagma Against All Heresies, a work On the Resurrection, a treatise against Marcion, and others. The Church has remembered him as Justin the Philosopher and Martyr, and his feast is kept on June 1.

Major Works

Three works survive intact, each a landmark of its kind: the earliest detailed Christian appeal to a Roman emperor, the earliest extended Christian response to a specific local persecution, and the earliest extensive Christian-Jewish dialogue. Together they invented several genres of Christian writing at once.

First Apology

Ἀπολογία Αʹ

Addressed around 153 AD to the emperor Antoninus Pius, his sons, the Senate, and the Roman people. In sixty-eight chapters Justin defends Christians against the standard charges — atheism, immorality, political disloyalty — and argues that Christianity fulfills what Greek philosophy sought. Chapters 61–67 contain the earliest detailed external description of Christian Baptism and the Eucharist, naming them by their Greek names and describing the Sunday gathering, the readings, the prayers, and the distribution.

Second Apology

Ἀπολογία Βʹ

A shorter and more occasional piece, c. 153–155 AD, prompted by the recent execution of a Roman Christian woman and her catechist. The most condensed expression of Justin's distinctive theological move: that the σπερματικὸς λόγος scattered seeds of truth among pagan philosophers, so that Socrates and Heraclitus, living by reason, already lived in partial communion with Christ the full Logos.

Dialogue with Trypho

Πρὸς Τρύφωνα Ἰουδαῖον Διάλογος

A literary dialogue of 142 chapters, probably composed late in Justin's life (c. 155–160), staging a two-day conversation with a learned Jew named Trypho on the meaning of the Scriptures. The most sustained second-century Christian argument that the Hebrew Scriptures, read through Christ, are the Church's own book — and the single most influential early source for Christian typological exegesis of the Old Testament.

Theological Pillars

Justin's theology moves along one axis: Christ is the Logos in whom all reason participates. Four terms carry that axis into concrete theological work — cosmic Christology, a generous reading of pagan wisdom, a defense of Christianity as the philosophy that completes philosophy, and an exegesis that finds Christ in the figures of the Old Testament.

λόγος
logos
Word · Reason · Rational Principle

For Justin, Christ is the cosmic Λόγος — the divine Word who spoke through the prophets, illumines every rational being, and became flesh in Jesus. His Logos Christology is the theological bridge across which the faith can walk into the Greco-Roman world and speak without losing itself.

σπερματικὸς λόγος
spermatikos logos
Seed-Logos · Seminal Word

Justin's signature move, borrowed from Stoic cosmology and baptized. The Logos sows seeds of himself in every rational being, so that Socrates and Heraclitus, living by reason, already partly belonged to Christ. What pagan philosophy glimpsed in fragments is fully disclosed in the incarnate Word.

φιλοσοφία
philosophia
Philosophy · Love of Wisdom

Christianity, for Justin, is not the enemy of philosophy but its completion. He kept his philosopher's cloak after baptism as a deliberate sign: the Christian is the true philosopher, because only in Christ does the love of wisdom find the Wisdom it loves.

τύπος
typos
Type · Figure · Pattern

The Dialogue is above all a school of typological reading. Moses's outstretched arms at Rephidim prefigure the Cross; Isaac bearing the wood, the bronze serpent, Jonah in the fish — each is a τύπος of Christ. This hermeneutic hardens in Justin into a stable Orthodox way of reading the Old Testament as already Christian book.

"Those who lived with the Logos are Christians, even though they were reckoned atheists — as among the Greeks, Socrates and Heraclitus and those like them."

— First Apology 46.3

Why Justin Matters Today

Justin is the first Christian thinker to treat Greek philosophy as a serious dialogue partner rather than a rival to be repelled. His generosity is costly and precise: what Plato and the Stoics glimpsed is genuinely of Christ, but the fullness of Christ is not Plato or the Stoics. The move opens a door the Church has walked through ever since — wherever Christian theology engages honestly with non-Christian thought without collapsing into it, Justin is in the background.

His Logos Christology became structural for later Alexandrian theology and, through it, for Nicaea. Orthodox readers should note honestly that Justin's expressions sometimes carry a functional subordinationism — the Logos as "second in rank" after the Father (1 Apology 13) — which the Church's conciliar discernment at Nicaea and Constantinople later refined. This is not a failure of Justin but an early stage in the doctrinal development that his own Logos theology made possible. To read him now is to see the raw material on which later Fathers worked.

Above all, Justin fuses thought and witness. He is a philosopher who dies for his philosophy, and the coherence of the two makes him a model for the intellectual life of faith. He shows that rigorous engagement with the world's best arguments and readiness to die for Christ belong on the same page. His feast, June 1, keeps that fusion inside the Orthodox Church's year.
